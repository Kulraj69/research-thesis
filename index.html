Background & Motivation

Why Aqueous Zn-Ion Batteries?

  • Safe — non-flammable aqueous electrolyte
  • Cheap — abundant Zn, no Li dependency
  • High capacity — 820 mAh/g theoretical

The Bottleneck

  • HER degrades the anode
  • Dendrite growth → short circuits
  • Root cause: uncontrolled Zn²⁺ solvation shell
Engineering the Zn²⁺ primary solvation shell is the lever for improving AZIB performance.

Thermodynamic balance:

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

At high T, the entropy term can dominate → counter-intuitive behavior that Arrhenius models cannot capture

Thiourea as an Electrolyte Additive

Why Thiourea SC(NH₂)₂?

  • Soft S-donor → strong Zn²⁺ affinity (HSAB principle)
  • Adsorbs on Zn anode → passivation layer
  • Alters solvation shell → stable SEI
  • Suppresses HER, promotes smooth deposition
How do structure and dynamics of the Zn²⁺ complex change with temperature when thiourea is present?

280 → 320 K: Normal Arrhenius
Ea ≈ 15–18 kJ/mol — consistent with diffusion-limited processes

320 → 340 K: D decreases by 6.5%
The complex slows down despite higher temperature

Bare Zn²⁺ in water: D ≈ 0.70 × 10⁻⁵ cm²/s → the complex is ~60× slower, confirming a bulky coordination shell

Arrhenius Plot: ln(D) vs 1/T

The 340 K data point falls measurably below the Arrhenius line — a genuine, non-statistical deviation.

Key Observations

  • Peak position constant → Zn-S bond length unaffected by T
  • Peak height decreases with T → thermal disorder
  • g(r): 2.0 at 280 K → <1.0 at 340 K
  • Most thermally labile of all three coordination modes

Contrast: Zn–SO₄ and Zn–water RDFs are temperature-insensitive

RDF: Zn–SO₄ — The Surprise

g(r) ≈ 32

at r ≈ 0.17 nm

vs. Zn–water g(r) ≈ 5  |  Zn–thiourea g(r) ≈ 2

Revised Coordination Picture

Sulfate — not thiourea — is the dominant first-shell ligand at 2M ZnSO₄

The complex is better described as:
[Zn(SO₄)ₙ(H₂O)ₘ](2−2n)

Why?

  • Strong Coulombic: Zn²⁺(+2) ↔ SO₄²⁻(−2)
  • 1:1 ratio (150:150) vs. thiourea 10:150
  • Temperature-insensitive → thermally robust

The diffusion anomaly at 340 K most likely involves the Zn–SO₄ coordination environment rather than the Zn–thiourea shell.

Hypothesis 1: Entropy-Driven CN Increase

Thermodynamic Argument

For: Zn-complex + L ⇌ Zn-complex-L

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS = −RT ln K

If binding releases structured water (ΔS > 0), high T favors more ligands binding → larger complex → slower diffusion

External Validation

Ding et al. (2024): ΔCN = 0.24 from concentration perturbation alone at 298 K.
A temperature perturbation of 20 K with TΔS amplification is plausible to cause ≥ comparable shift.

Quantitative Check (Stokes-Einstein)

320 K → 340 K:

T increase+6.3%
η decrease−10%
D change−6.5%

∴ rH must increase by ~14%

+1 ligand gives ~5–6% → requires multiple ligands or dimerization

Hypotheses 2 & 3

H2: SO₄²⁻-Bridged Aggregation

Elevated Probability

Given g(r) ≈ 32 for Zn–SO₄, sulfate-bridged dimerization is highly plausible at high T:

[Zn₂(SO₄)y(H₂O)x]

Stokes-Einstein: dimer → ~21% slower
(D ∝ V⁻¹/³, dimer V = 2×monomer)

Only ~30% partial dimerization needed to explain the 6.5% decrease

Test: Zn–Zn RDF at 340 K

H3: H-Bond Network Reorganization

Above ~320 K, bulk water H-bond network restructures significantly

  • Enhanced water structuring around complex
  • Higher local viscosity
  • Could reduce D without size change

Combined Mechanism

These three hypotheses are not mutually exclusive. The actual mechanism likely involves contributions from all three.

Conclusions

1  Validated MD model — Zn-S: 2.2–2.3 Å (exp: 2.3–2.4 Å), density, temperature, energy all verified
2  Non-Arrhenius anomaly discovered — D increases normally 280→320 K, then decreases 6.5% at 340 K
3  Revised coordination picture — SO₄²⁻ is the dominant 1st-shell ligand (g(r) ≈ 32), not thiourea (g(r) ≈ 2)
4  Three mechanisms proposed — entropy-driven CN increase + SO₄ bridging (elevated) + H-bond restructuring
5  Practical implication — Arrhenius extrapolation fails for high-T AZIB electrolyte design
